Stop Rate

The stop rate is the proportion of drivers stopped compaired to the population of the city. For all cities the stop rate is higher for men.

Search Rate

The search rate is the proportion of stopped drivers that have been searched. It is represented for the search of the car as well as the serch of the person. We observe higher person search rates on women.

Hit Rate

The hit rate is the proportion of searched drivers for which contraband (drugs, guns) has between found. We performed a statistical Fisher test to assess if the proportion of men carrying contraband among all searched men is significantly different than the one of women. The lighter the color of the dot, the high the significance. With the exception of Greensboro we observe higher it reates for men. However, only one is significantly different than women's hit rate. It seems not justified to seach men more than women.

Search and arrestation/citation decision

Equal treatment for male and female drivers having committed the same infraction ?

To assess if men and women are treated equally, we matched them in pairs and compared the decision taken by the police officer. The matching allows to pair one male driver with one female driver presenting a similar subjective signal to the police officer. Men-Women pairs have the same race to avoid a potential racial bias. They belong to the same age category (more or less than 45 years old). The age is used as a proxy for the attractiveness of the drivers.

When analyzing the search decision, the pairs have also the same reason for search which allows to compare drivers offering to the officer a similar suspicion level to find contraband.

When analyzing the stop outcome decision, the pairs have also the same reason for stop and the same search issue (contraband found or not). This allow to compare drivers having committed the same infractions.

The results are displayed on the maps bellow.

SEARCH DECISION

The map below presents the average outcome difference (AOD) in the personal search decision between men and women. If the driver is searched, the outcome is set to 1 and kept to 0 otherwise. The difference is computed as the outcome for men minus the outcome for women. A positive value indicates that men are more searched. For all the states analyzed, the difference is significantly positive (95% confidence intervals can be displayed by clicking on the map). Men appear to be more searched than women for similar suspicion degree to be carrying contraband.

ARRESTATION/CITATION DECISION

The map below presents the average outcome difference (AOD) in the arrestation/citation decision. If the driver has been arrested or a citation has been issued the outcome is set to 1 (severe decision). If the driver receives a warning only, the outcome is set to 0. Except for police departments of North Carolina and Tennessee, the AOD is significantly negative (95% confidence intervals can be displayed by clicking on the map). For these cases, police officers tend to let women go more easily than men for the same infraction.

When the driver is searched but not his/her car...

When a police officer is suspecting the driver to be carrying contraband, he can search him/her and his/her car. While looking for drugs or guns it would be surprising to search the person but not the car. These cases could be instances of unwarranted person searches and sexual assault.

The figure bellow compares the AOD in the search decision of stops where the car has been searched to stops where it wasn’t. We observe AODs of 0 for some police departments. These cases are specific to the dataset. The search reason feature refers also to the person search meaning that by matching pairs on the search reason we have only drivers that have been searched when their car wasn’t. For the datasets of Charlotte and Greensboro, we observe lower AOD for cases where the car was searched than where it wasn’t. The difference between men and women is then smaller.

Case study on Florida state : influence of the officers' gender on the stop outcome decision

Let's dig into even more details concerning stop decisions. What happens if we split female from male officers, and look for the average outcome difference ? The aim of this analysis is to find out if the officer's gender has an impact on a potential bias in decision making. For this purpose, a similar methodology is applied as before to balance our dataset, and outcome difference for each type of potential violation will be compared. Very few datasets contain all information needed for this analysis, hence it is performed only on the big dataset of Florida state (3.2 Mio stop records for men officers, 0.25 Mio for women officers before filtering).

The AOD for the woman officers is near 0, and the confidence interval ranges from negative to positive values:

at this point, it is not possible to determine whether woman officers take biased decisions. However, it seems different for men : their average outcome when deciding on the outcome of a stop seems to indicate that they tend to search men more than woman drivers, even if the age range, race, subjective indicator (reason for stop) are identical for them.


From the results when splitting men from women officers, and splitting the group by type of reason, some interesting findings appear :

- Men officers are more gentle with female drivers, reprehending them less than male drivers, whereas women officers do not make a significant difference between them. The exact inverse conclusion can be drawn for the seatbelt violation.

- Both men and women officers seem more gentle with female drivers that have faulty equipment, as well as other types of TAG or registration violations.

- Men officers seem particularly biased regarding "Other type of moving violation" : hence men officers decide themselves for harder outcomes when facing a male driver. This is however not the case for female officers, acting equally on male and female drivers for this violation type.

- Female officers seem particularly biased with female for traffic control device violation : they tend to let them get away more easily than men drivers.

Conclusion

Meat consumption is an important consideration to tackle climate change. We have looked into meat consumption in Europe and France, and its association with different factors that could impact consumption. We have made the following observations:

    - We have seen that wealth and economic considerations play a role in determining people's consumption, but mostly in the long term, and that a fast change in wealth does not quickly affect meat consumption.

    - We have seen that in France, the drop in meat consumption in 2008 is more likely related to the economic situation than to the price of meat.

    - That in France at least, meat consumption does not seem to be related to interest in climate change.

    - That people's interest in climate change is fairly related to news coverage.
